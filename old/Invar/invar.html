Invar effect studied using time-resolved x-ray diffraction

Most recently, I've been studying the Invar effect using time resolved X-ray diffraction at Advanced Photon Source in Chicago. I still got a lot of data analysis to do and this is just a preview of the full work.

The invar effect can be found in FeNi alloys (you may find it in other alloys, but FeNi is the one I'm focused on).

Invar alloys have the property to have a extremely low coefficient of thermal expansion for a big range of temperature; In other words, the material does not expand nor contracts when exposed to temperature changes.

The experiment was performed using a pump and probe technique. Pulses of laser, 800nm, were used as pump and x-rays pulses as probe. The laser would heat up the sample and a short period of time later the x-ray would hit the sample, diffracting while the sample is still exctied.

We discorvered that for short periods of time (of order of pico seconds) the invar effect is not seen.

As you may seen through the graph below, which is a graph, in a log scale, of the difference of intensity for a certain Bragg peak between a normal peak and an excited peak as a function of the delay between the laser and the x-ray.

In short periods of time, the difference is relatively big, which means that the Bragg peak in question has moved (changed position) due to photon excitation. This changing caracterizes a expansion of the unity cell.

After a time of 10-8 seconds the sample starts "healing" and after 10-6 seconds it is "healed", characterizing the Invar effect.

The different colors means different laser power.
